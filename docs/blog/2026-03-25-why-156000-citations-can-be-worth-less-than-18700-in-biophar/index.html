Why 156,000 Citations Can Be Worth Less Than 18,700 in Biopharma

The figure reflects the paper’s methodology and sample construction; citation values are most comparable within each sub-sample.A new working paper suggests that markets often reward narrative clarity
March 25, 2026

A new working paper suggests that markets often reward narrative clarity more than scientific spillovers.

By Po-Sung(Sinclair) Huang

We like to think capital markets reward great science.

In biopharma, that is only partly true.

In a new working paper, I compare market capitalisation with forward citations — a proxy for how much others are actually using a firm’s research. What emerges is not a smooth relationship, but a striking pricing gap.

Consider two firms.

Eisai generated roughly 156,000 forward citations, yet its market capitalisation was under $9 billion.

Argenx generated about 18,700 forward citations, yet its market capitalisation exceeded $42 billion.

That does not prove markets are irrational. But it does suggest something more structural: markets do not price scientific knowledge evenly. They also price visibility, narrative compression, business-model clarity, geography, and investor preference for convexity.

To make that asymmetry visible, I use a simple metric:

Innovation Premium Multiplier (IPM) = Market capitalization ÷ Forward citations

In plain English, IPM asks a straightforward question:

How much market value are investors assigning to one unit of downstream knowledge use?

A high IPM suggests that investors are assigning a rich valuation to each unit of scientific impact. A very low IPM suggests that a firm may be generating substantial knowledge spillovers without receiving proportionate market recognition.

A methodological noteThe underlying paper uses two internally consistent sub-samples rather than a single fully harmonised global dataset. That matters. Forward citation levels are most comparable within each sub-sample rather than mechanically across all firms in the figure. In other words, the strongest comparisons are within each sample’s internal structure, while the combined figure is best read as a map of valuation asymmetry rather than a perfect apples-to-apples ranking across the full universe.#

Also, “forward citations” in this paper refer to OpenAlex-indexed downstream citations to a firm’s research output. In the non-U.S. sample, the measure is based on cumulative citations to the firm’s 2015–2024 R&D works. In the U.S. sample, the paper uses an internally consistent all-time institution-level approach for a separate set of firms. That is precisely why within-subsample comparison matters.

What the chart showsWhen market capitalisation is plotted against forward citations on log scales, the first thing that stands out is not a neat linear hierarchy. It is vertical dispersion.#

Firms with broadly similar levels of scientific influence can sit at very different valuation levels.

Across the combined sample of 92 firms, forward citations do have a statistically significant positive association with market capitalisation, but they explain only about 46% of the cross-sectional variation. In other words, scientific influence matters, but it is far from sufficient to explain how markets price biopharma firms.

That unexplained space is where narrative, governance, geography, and investor risk preference appear to do much of the real pricing work.

Eisai vs. Argenx: a pricing gap, not necessarily a science gapThe cleanest example is Eisai versus Argenx.#

In the paper’s global non-U.S. sub-sample, Eisai generated about 156,488 forward citations and had a market capitalisation of roughly $8.998 billion, implying an IPM of about 0.058. Argenx generated about 18,719 forward citations and a market capitalisation of roughly $42.376 billion, implying an IPM of about 2.264.

That means a unit of knowledge generated by Argenx is priced at nearly 39 times the value of a unit generated by Eisai. Eisai produced about 8.4 times more forward citations, yet investors rewarded it far less per unit of downstream scientific impact.

A natural objection is that perhaps the “cheap” knowledge is simply lower quality.

But that is not what the paper suggests.

One of the reasons Eisai is analytically interesting is that its citation network appears to be tied to institutions deeply embedded in the infrastructure of global health research. In the paper, this serves as a quality falsification check: the issue is unlikely to be that Eisai’s scientific output is peripheral or unused. The more plausible interpretation is that the market is discounting a type of knowledge that is diffuse, foundational, and harder to compress into a simple equity story.

Inside the U.S.: the Large Pharma Knowledge DiscountThe pattern does not stop at the international level. It reappears inside the U.S. sample.#

Large pharma firms such as Pfizer, Merck, Amgen, and Eli Lilly generate citation volumes measured in the millions, yet the paper finds that U.S. Large Pharma has a median IPM of about 0.036, compared with 0.660 for U.S. Biotechs — a gap of nearly 18 times.

That is why I think of the phenomenon as a Large Pharma Knowledge Discount.

A smaller biotech with a narrow but highly visible clinical story can receive a much richer premium per unit of knowledge use than a large, scientifically foundational company whose research has shaped entire therapeutic fields.

This is not simply a Japan discount story, nor merely a cross-border anomaly. The paper’s U.S. evidence suggests that the same structural logic can operate even within a single national market.

Why does this happen?My reading is that three structural forces are doing much of the work.#

The first is narrative visibility. Markets prefer stories they can compress. A focused company with one or two lead assets can be translated into a familiar growth narrative: one pipeline, one catalyst path, one acquisition possibility, one valuation frame. That kind of story is easy to underwrite.

The second is geography and governance. In the global sample, the paper finds clear regional stratification in IPM levels, with Japanese firms clustering at the low end despite high citation intensity. That is consistent with a world in which scientific quality may be global, but the market’s willingness to reward that science remains shaped by governance norms, capital allocation expectations, and geography-specific discounting.

The third is asymmetric risk preference. Biotech capital is structurally drawn to convexity. Investors will often pay aggressively for a smaller, more binary story because the upside is legible and potentially explosive. Large pharma, by contrast, is more likely to be treated as a mature, diversified, almost defensive asset, even when its underlying knowledge platform is vastly more consequential.

Why this mattersIPM is not a magic number.#

But it may be a useful way to screen for a very specific phenomenon: the gap between knowledge creation and value capture.

For investors, low-IPM, high-citation firms may represent under-recognised knowledge platforms.

For corporate strategy and BD teams, IPM may help identify where narrative repositioning — or transaction structure — could unlock value that public markets are not fully recognising.

For M&A, the spread between low-IPM incumbents and high-IPM focused firms offers a way to think about how markets may be underpricing broad scientific platforms before they are repackaged into a cleaner commercial story.

The broader questionThe conventional assumption in innovation economics is that better science should eventually lead to better value capture.#

This paper suggests that the relationship is much messier.

Markets do reward science.

But they do not reward all science equally.

They reward science that can be narrated, packaged, localised, and monetised in ways capital can quickly understand.

The rest may still matter enormously — for medicine, for public health, for research infrastructure, and for future breakthroughs — while remaining only partially visible in price.

That is the gap IPM is trying to make visible.
